The Line Graphs comes into picture when you have Data, which is in the form of series - Single series, two line series or multiple series.
Use Case: Line graphs are used to track changes over short and long periods of time.
Bar
Bar GraphÂ is one of the widely used graphs.
These graphs are easily readable. One can easily:
Compare the endpoints of the bars.
Understand which category is smallest and biggest.
Measure the incremental difference between two categories.
Use Case: Bar graphs are used to compare things between different groups or to track changes over time.
Good practices
Always start the Bar graphs with Zero baseline.
Make sure that Bars are wider than the white space between the bars.
Bars should neither be too thin nor too thick; they should be of right width.
Commonly used Bar graphs are
Vertical Bar
Stacked Vertical Bar
Horizontal Bar
Stacked Horizontal Bar
Vertical Bars
Like Line bars, You can find single series, Two series and Multiple series of Vertical bars, These are used for showcasing categorical data.
Stacked Vertical Bars
These are generally used for comparing categories; colors are used to highlight the comparison. However, the use cases for stacked vertical bars are very limited. The example illustrating stacked vertical bar is displayed above.
Horizontal Bars
These are similar to Vertical Bars that have single series, Two series, and multiple series.
Categories that have very long names can be represented using Horizontal bars.
These graphs are extremely easy to read. Maintain the categories in an ordered form, highlighting focused data from top to bottom fashion.
Stacked Horizontal Bars
These are similar to stacked vertical bars where you can show the totals across different categories and also give sense to the subcomponent pieces.
These can be very well structured to show either absolute values or sum to 100%.

Graphs that have to be Avoided
There are few specific graphs and elements that need to be avoided, they are:
Pie chart
Donut chart
3D
Secondary Y - Axis
These graphs create a lot of visual confusion.
Let us now try to understand with an example:
The Pie chart shows market share across four suppliers: A, B, C, and D. If you take the first look on the pie chart and you ask your audience a question, 'which supplier has the largest share?'.
You would get an answer as Supplier B (light green). But the fact isÂ Supplier A is largest with 34%Â share whereasÂ Supplier B is just 31%.
Hence, avoid using these kinds of charts which create visual confusion.
